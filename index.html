Chapter 6: The Carbon Cycle

Understanding the Carbon Cycle: The carbon cycle is a fundamental natural process that circulates carbon through Earth’s atmosphere, biosphere, geosphere, and hydrosphere. As a key element for life, carbon helps regulate Earth’s climate and sustains ecosystems.

This continuous movement of carbon occurs between living organisms, the air, soil, oceans, and fossil fuels, playing a crucial role in maintaining environmental balance.

How the Carbon Cycle Works

  • Photosynthesis: Plants absorb carbon dioxide (CO₂) from the atmosphere, converting it into oxygen and organic matter.
  • Respiration: Animals and plants release CO₂ back into the atmosphere through breathing and the breakdown of organic material.
  • Decomposition: Dead organisms decay, returning carbon to the soil and releasing CO₂.
  • Ocean Absorption: Oceans function as carbon sinks, absorbing CO₂ from the air.
  • Fossil Fuel Combustion: Over time, buried carbon forms fossil fuels. When burned, these fuels release CO₂, contributing to rising atmospheric carbon levels.
Fast Fact
Soils store three times more carbon than the atmosphere. Healthy soils are essential in slowing climate change.

The Human Impact

Burning Fossil Fuels: Human activities, such as industrial processes and transportation, release excess CO₂, intensifying climate change.

Deforestation: Removing forests reduces carbon absorption, increasing greenhouse gases in the atmosphere.

Agriculture & Soil Degradation: Certain farming practices can deplete carbon-rich soil, releasing stored carbon into the air.

Tip
Support sustainable forestry and regenerative agriculture to keep carbon in the ground.

Why It Matters & How We Can Help

  • Reduce fossil fuel consumption: Shift to renewable energy like solar and wind.
  • Reforest and conserve natural carbon sinks: Forests, wetlands, and grasslands.
  • Invest in carbon capture: Extract CO₂ from the air for long-term storage or repurposing.
